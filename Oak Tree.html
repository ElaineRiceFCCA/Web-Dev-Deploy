Oak Tree

The quintessential Irish oak variety is the sessile oak, predominant in the country's most iconic woodlands. Often thriving in poor acid soils, particularly in hilly regions, these woodlands grace locations like Killarney in Co. Kerry, the Glen of the Downs in Co. Wicklow, and Glenveagh in Co. Donegal.

These oak woodlands serve as vital ecological habitats, supporting a plethora of invertebrate species, as well as numerous birds and mammals. The term "sessile" denotes that the acorns lack a stalk, distinguishing them from the pedunculate oak whose acorns hang from lengthy stalks.

Despite once being widespread across Ireland, extensive centuries-old harvesting practices, coupled with inadequate tree replacement efforts, have rendered truly native oak specimens increasingly rare, though remnants can still be found in small woodlands scattered across various counties.

Semi-natural oak woodlands often feature a mix of birch and ash, with hazel, holly, and rowan interspersed throughout the undergrowth. Oaks have long been prized for their premium timber, renowned for its aesthetic appeal and robustness, making it a favored material for furniture production.

While oak male flowers are inconspicuous catkins that precede the emergence of leaves, the seeds-acorns are far more conspicuous. Oak trees do not yield a bountiful crop every year, underscoring the value of gathering plentiful acorns during favorable seasons.
