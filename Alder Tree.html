Alder Tree

Alders, among Ireland's most traditional and widely spread trees, thrive in damp habitats, lining the shores of freshwater loughs and meandering riverbanks where their robust, fibrous roots aid in soil retention.

Notable woodlands can be found in Ross Island, Killarney, County Kerry, and the Gearagh, County Cork, while Grantstown Wood, County Laois, stands as a rare example of wet woodland on alkaline soil.

Like many trees, alders bloom before leaf emergence, displaying captivating reddish catkins and small cones containing seeds. They adapt well to various soil types but particularly favor moist environments. Given fertile, damp soil, alders exhibit rapid growth, making them highly productive for timber. In ancient Ireland, sections of alder trunks served as round shields, later becoming prized for crafting clogs and furniture, earning them the nickname "Irish mahogany."

Due to its resistance to decay when submerged, alder was used in the construction of sluice gates and other water-retaining structures along streams, rivers, and canals.
