Willow Tree

Ireland boasts several native varieties of willow, all thriving in moist soil and characterized by their catkins or "pussy willows," which bear seeds and are easily propagated from cuttings due to their high rooting propensity.

Among the most prevalent species are the goat willow, the rusty or grey willow (referred to as "sallies"), and the eared willow. While typically found in damp environments, the goat willow can also establish itself in rough or disturbed terrain in dryer regions. Additionally, the bay-leaved willow, distinguished by its glossy green foliage, thrives alongside small rivers and ditches.

Osiers, recognized for their long, slender leaves, do not mature into towering trees. Historically, they were cultivated and pruned to the base to stimulate the growth of long, flexible shoots utilized in basket weaving. Today, this species is often cultivated for biomass production, offering a sustainable energy source.

Willow trees support a diverse insect population, making them an essential food source for birds, particularly during the summer months, a notable example being the willow warbler.

Interestingly, willow trees have been historically associated with healing properties. Willow bark contains a compound called salicin, which is similar to aspirin and has been used for centuries to alleviate pain and reduce fever.
