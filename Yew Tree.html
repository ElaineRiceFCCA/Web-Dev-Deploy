Yew Tree

The yew tree, a native species, thrives in old woodlands but is also commonly found in the cultivated environments of estates and churchyards. With its dark foliage and vibrant red berries enveloping a single seed, the yew presents a striking presence. Notably, Reenadina wood on the Muckross Peninsula, Co. Kerry, stands as Ireland’s sole native yew woodland.

A unique variant of the Irish yew, known as Taxus baccata ‘fastigata’, boasts an upright growth pattern and was initially discovered on rocky limestone hills in Co. Fermanagh. This variant was later cultivated at Florencecourt and subsequently introduced into numerous gardens and churchyards. While many yew trees exhibit single-sex characteristics, the majority of Irish yews are female, bearing fruit albeit with slow germination rates.

The most viable yew seeds are those consumed by birds and subsequently passed through their digestive systems; such exposed seeds can often be gathered from beneath yew trees. Additionally, there exist ornamental garden varieties of yew, some featuring yellow fruit or golden foliage, which are typically propagated through cuttings.

Yew trees thrive in well-drained sites, preferably sheltered from excessive wind and frost. However, it's important to note that both the leaves and seeds of yew trees are toxic to most livestock and can pose risks to animals and children if planted in areas accessible to them. Despite this, yew trees serve as valuable habitats for wildlife, with birds frequently roosting and nesting among their branches.
