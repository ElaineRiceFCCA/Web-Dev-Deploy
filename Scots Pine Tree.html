Scots Pine Tree

Originally, the Scots pine was native to Ireland, as evidenced by pollen found in soil samples from bogs, indicating its widespread presence thousands of years ago.

However, due to human impact and the gradual shift towards a warmer, wetter climate, the Scots pine experienced a decline, and there are indications that it may have vanished completely from the region.

Remarkably, pine stumps dating back 7,000 years have been discovered in bogs, standing as remnants of a time before the formation of peat. Nowadays, most of the Scots pines scattered across the Irish countryside were imported from Scotland and planted within the last 150 years.

Efforts have been underway to reintroduce this once-native species, as it holds ecological significance. Scots pine can thrive in marginal lands where other tree species struggle, and it offers quicker growth and more versatile wood compared to broadleaf trees.

Despite being a coniferous species, Scots pine contributes to biodiversity by providing habitat for various wildlife species, even as canopy cover changes. Notably, our native red squirrels show a preference for the seeds of Scots pine over other options.
